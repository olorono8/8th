üîé Introduction

Force is a push or pull upon an object resulting from its interaction with another object. It can cause an object to start moving, stop moving, or change direction.

Force: A push or pull acting upon an object.

‚öôÔ∏è Types of Forces

  • Contact Forces: Forces that require physical contact (e.g., friction, applied force).
  • Non-contact Forces: Forces that act at a distance without contact (e.g., gravitational force, magnetic force).

üí° Effects of Force

  • Change the shape of an object (e.g., squeezing a rubber ball).
  • Change the speed of an object (speed up or slow down).
  • Change the direction of an object.
  • Start or stop the motion of an object.

üìê Measuring Force

Force is measured using a device called a spring balance. The SI unit of force is the Newton (N).

‚öñÔ∏è Pressure

Pressure is defined as the force applied per unit area on a surface.

Pressure (P) = Force (F) / Area (A)

Where,

  • P = Pressure (in pascal, Pa)
  • F = Force applied (in newton, N)
  • A = Area on which force is applied (in square meters, m¬≤)

üîç Effects of Pressure

  • A smaller area of contact results in greater pressure for the same force (e.g., nails have sharp points to exert more pressure and pierce objects).
  • A larger area of contact reduces pressure (e.g., snowshoes help distribute weight and reduce pressure on snow).

üíº Applications of Pressure

  • Sharp knives and needles: Concentrate force on small areas to increase pressure and cut easily.
  • Snowshoes: Increase area of contact with snow to decrease pressure, preventing sinking.
  • High heels vs. flat shoes: High heels exert more pressure and can damage floors.
  • Hydraulic machines: Use pressure to lift heavy loads.

üìè Pressure in Fluids

Pressure exerted by fluids (liquids and gases) acts equally in all directions. It increases with depth in a liquid.

üåü Key Points to Remember

  • Force is a push or pull acting on an object.
  • Force can cause change in motion or shape.
  • Pressure is force per unit area.
  • Pressure increases if force increases or area decreases.
  • Pressure exerted by fluids acts in all directions.
  • Tools like knives and needles use pressure to work effectively.
