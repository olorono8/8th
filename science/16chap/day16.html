üí° Introduction

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects. It travels in straight lines and is essential for vision and many other processes.

üî¶ Sources of Light

  • Natural sources: The Sun, stars, fireflies.
  • Artificial sources: Bulbs, candles, LEDs, torches.
  • Objects that emit their own light are called luminous.
  • Objects that do not emit light but reflect it are called non-luminous.

‚û°Ô∏è Propagation of Light

Light travels in a straight line. This can be observed through shadows and the pinhole camera experiment.

  • Shadows: Formed when an opaque object blocks light.
  • Pinhole camera: A simple device that forms an inverted image using light passing through a small hole.

üîÑ Reflection of Light

When light falls on a surface and bounces back, it is called reflection.

Reflection: The bouncing back of light from a surface.
  • The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.
  • Plane mirrors produce virtual, erect images.
  • Reflection helps in making mirrors, periscopes, and other optical devices.

üîç Image Formation by Plane Mirror

  • The image is laterally inverted (left and right are reversed).
  • The image is virtual (cannot be obtained on a screen).
  • The image is of the same size as the object.
  • The image is as far behind the mirror as the object is in front.
üì∏ Light rays reflecting off a plane mirror to form an image.

üîé Uses of Mirrors

  • Plane mirrors are used in dressing tables and shaving mirrors.
  • Concave mirrors are used in torches, headlights, and shaving mirrors for magnified images.
  • Convex mirrors are used in vehicle side mirrors for a wider field of view.

üåü Key Points to Remember

  • Light travels in a straight line.
  • Reflection follows the law: angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
  • Plane mirrors produce virtual, erect, and same-sized images.
  • Objects can be luminous or non-luminous.
  • Mirrors have many practical applications.
